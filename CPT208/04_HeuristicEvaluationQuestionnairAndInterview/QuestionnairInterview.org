** How to design a good questionnair
1. Question Order
   Question order matters because earlier questions may influence how participants interpret and answer later questions.
   the correct sequence is to ask the basic background first, then general experience, and then specific feedback, then open questions;
2. different versions for different populations, 
   Different versions of a questionnaire may be needed for different populations because different user groups have different roles, tasks, and experiences.
3. Provide clear instrcutions,
   Different versions of a questionnaire may be needed for different populations because different user groups have different roles, tasks, and experiences.
4. avoid long questions/questionnair,
   Very long questions and questionnaires should be avoided because they increase participant fatigue and may reduce response quality.
5. positive/negative/mixed wording, 
   Researchers should decide whether questionnaire items will be all positive, all negative, or mixed, because wording direction can affect how participants respond.
   for the all positive or all negative questionnairs, the user may have a bias to answer all the question in the same way
   but for mixed questionnairs, the user may choose the answer more carefully , but sometimes the user may be confused by the different wording and then give a wrong answer.
6. white space vs compactness
   Questionnaire layout should balance white space and compactness so that it is readable without becoming unnecessarily long.

*** Bad Question Examples: 
1. Double-Barrelled Questions
   e.g. Is the app useful and enjoyable?
   analysis: the question is double-barrelled becuase it asks about two different aspects (usefulness and enjoyability)
   we should split it into two separate questions: "Is the app useful?" and "Is the app enjoyable?"
   "This is a double-barrelled question because it asks about two different aspects at the same time. It should be split into two separate questions."
2. Leading questions
   e.g. How helpful was this excellent feature?
   analysis: the question is leading because it uses the word "excellent" which may bias the respondent's answer.
   we should change it into rating question like "How would you rate this feature"
   "This is a leading question because it suggests a preferred answer. It should be rewritten in a neutral way."
3. Jargon:
   e.g. "How do you evaluate the information *architecture* of the app?"
   some user do not know waht is the "information architecture".
   we should change it into "Was it easy to find the information you needed?"
   "The qeustion uses jargon that participants may not understand. It should use simple langualge ralated to participates' actual experience."
4. Options not mutuallly exclusive:
   e.g. "How long didi you use the app? [] 0-1 hours [] 1-2 hours [] 2-3 hours"

* Interview
** Types of Interview
Unstructured interviews are flexible and can produce rich data, but they are difficult to replicate and compare across participants.
1. Unstructured Interview:
   no fixed questionns, just like chatting.
   - pros:
     very rich, flexible, can explore unexpected topics
   - cons: hard to
     replicate, compare, analyze
     depends heavily on interviewer's skills
2. Structured
   Structured interviews use a fixed set of questions in a fixed order. They are more replicable and easier to compare, but less flexible and less rich.
   - pros: easy
     compare, replicable, analyze
   - cons: less
     flexible, rich, hard to explore unexpected topics
3. Semi-structured
   "most common interview type"
   Semi-structured interviews use a prepared set of questions but allow the interviewer to ask follow-up questions. They balance comparability with flexibility and are useful for exploring participants' experiences in depth.
   pros: balance structurea and death into interview, allow to explore unexpected topics, easier to compare than unstructured, more flexible than structured
4. Focus group
   A focus group is a group interview where participants discuss a topic together. It can reveal shared opinions and disagreements, but dominant participants and social pressure may influence the data.
   - cons:
     participants can respond to each other's ideas
     can reveal group opinions
     efficient for collecting multiple views
   - pros:
     dominant participants may control discussion
     some people may stay silent
     social pressure may affect answers
     less suitable for sensitive topics
** Interview Questions:
two types: *CLOSED* and *OPEN* questions
- e.g. good questions: 
  Can you describe what happened when you tried to use this feature?
  What did you expect to happen?
  Why did you choose that option?
  What problems did you encounter?
- e.g. Bad Questions:
  - long questions: user are easy to forgot the detail of question if too long. sol. split it into multiple short questions
  - compound questions: like "Was the app useful and easy to learn?" sol. split it into two separate questions
  - jargon: make it hard for user to understand. sol. use simple language related to user's experience
  - leading questions: like "How helpful was this excellent feature?" sol. rewrite in a neutral way like "How would you rate this feature?"
  - unconscious bias:
    Do not ask questions that reflect the interviewer's asumptions or stereotypes. 
    like "Do older users find this interview difficult?" -> "What difficulties, if any, did you experience when using this interface?"
Good interview questions should be clear, neutral, and easy to understand. Researchers should avoid long questions, compound questions, jargon, leading questions, and unconscious bias.

** use prototype and scenario to help interview
The interview process can be enriched by using prompts such as prototypes, scenarios, or example tasks. These help participants discuss concrete experiences and provide more specific feedback.

** Runing the Interview:
A typical interview includes introduction, warm-up, main body, cool-off period, and closure. The interviewer should explain the purpose and ethical issues, build rapport, ask questions in a logical order, reduce tension at the end, and thank the participant.
1. Introduction: intro myself. intro the propose of the interview, claim the ethical issues, ask for permission to record; show the informed consent form;
2. Warm-up: chill the interviewee, establish rapport.
3. Main body: ask the question in logical order, from normal to specific,  make a detailed inquiry if needed;
4. Cool-off period: reduce the stress of the interviewee through asking some simple questions.
5. Closure: Appriciate the interviewee, claim that the interview is over (like turn off the recording)

* Questionnair vs Interview
  Questionnaires are suitable for collecting data from a large number of participants at relatively low cost. They are good for quantitative data and closed-ended questions, but they often provide shallow data and offer limited opportunities for follow-up.
Interviews are suitable for collecting deeper qualitative data. They allow researchers to ask follow-up questions and explore participants' experiences, motivations, and reasons. However, they are more time-consuming, require interviewer skill, and usually involve fewer participants.
